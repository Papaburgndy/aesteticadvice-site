Sunscreen FAQ

Mineral vs chemical sunscreen — which is better?
Neither is universally better. Mineral sunscreens (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) sit on top of skin and physically block UV — better for sensitive skin and reef-safe. Chemical sunscreens absorb UV and convert it to heat — typically more elegant texture and no white cast. Both protect equally when applied correctly.
Do I need SPF 50 or is SPF 30 enough?
SPF 30 blocks 97% of UVB rays; SPF 50 blocks 98%. The difference is small but matters for high-exposure situations (beach, skiing, outdoor work). For daily office-to-car-to-office use, SPF 30 applied generously is fine. When in doubt, go higher.
How much sunscreen should I apply?
Most people apply 20–50% of the recommended amount. For face and neck, you need about half a teaspoon (roughly a nickel-sized amount). Most SPF testing uses 2mg/cm² — less than that and you don't get the labeled protection.
Does sunscreen cause vitamin D deficiency?
Research consistently shows that daily sunscreen use does not cause vitamin D deficiency in practice, because most people don't apply it perfectly and some UV still reaches the skin. If concerned, vitamin D supplements are a reliable solution.

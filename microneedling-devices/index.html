🔬 How to Choose a Microneedling Device

📏 Needle Depth Guide

0.1–0.3mm: product absorption enhancement, no downtime. 0.5mm: surface remodeling, mild redness 24h. 1.0mm: collagen stimulation, 24–48h downtime. 1.5mm+: acne scars and stretch marks, 48–72h recovery. Stay at 0.5mm or below for at-home use.

🔩 Roller vs Pen

Pens create perpendicular channels through stamping motion — more precise, less epidermal tearing. Rollers create angled channels through rolling — easier to use but the tearing pattern is less favorable. Pens are clinically preferred, especially for sensitive areas.

🧼 Sterilization Is Non-Negotiable

Disinfect your device with 70% isopropyl alcohol before and after every use. Replace cartridges/needles after each session. Using a dull or contaminated needle causes more tearing than channeling and dramatically increases infection risk.

💧 The Serum Window

The 24–72 hours post-microneedling are when your skin is most receptive to actives. Apply hyaluronic acid, peptides, growth factors, and vitamin C during this window. Avoid retinol, AHAs, and vitamin C (ascorbic acid) immediately post-treatment.

Microneedling FAQs

At 0.25–0.5mm depth: once per week is appropriate. At 1.0mm+: no more than once per month to allow full collagen remodeling between sessions. Over-microneedling causes chronic inflammation and can worsen texture rather than improve it.
Hyaluronic acid (pure hydration, no irritation risk), peptides (growth factors signal repair), and niacinamide (soothing, barrier support) are ideal immediately post-microneedling. Avoid retinol, AHAs, BHAs, and vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid) for 24–48 hours.
Done correctly at the right depth, microneedling is one of the most effective treatments for atrophic acne scars. Done incorrectly — with contaminated needles, too much pressure, or active breakouts — it can spread bacteria and worsen scarring. Never microneedle over active acne.
Microneedling pens create a stamping motion that forms perpendicular channels with less epidermal tearing than the angled channels of rollers. This is clinically preferred. Pens are also easier to control depth precisely and work better around the nose and orbital area.

At-Home Chemical Peels: The Complete Safety Guide (AHA, BHA, TCA)

By AesteticAdvice Team · 2026-03-02 · 10 min read

Chemical Peels at Home: Powerful but Unforgiving

Chemical peels work by applying an acid solution that intentionally injures the outer layers of skin, forcing your body to shed damaged cells and generate fresh, new skin. Done right, they dramatically improve texture, fade hyperpigmentation, unclog pores, and reduce fine lines. Done wrong, they cause burns, permanent scarring, and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation that takes months to resolve.

This is the guide that tells you the full truth — not just the marketing version.

The Three Main Types of At-Home Peels

AHA Peels (Alpha Hydroxy Acids)

AHAs include glycolic acid (most potent, smallest molecule), lactic acid (gentler, good for sensitive skin), mandelic acid (gentlest, large molecule, great for beginners and darker skin tones), and malic acid. They work on the surface of skin, dissolving the bonds between dead skin cells. Best for: uneven texture, dullness, fine lines, mild hyperpigmentation.

  • Beginner range: 5–10% glycolic or lactic acid (leave on for 1–3 minutes)
  • Intermediate: 20–30% glycolic (leave on for 2–5 minutes max)
  • Advanced at-home: 40–50% glycolic (approach with extreme caution, patch test mandatory)

BHA Peels (Beta Hydroxy Acids)

Salicylic acid is the main BHA. It's oil-soluble, which means it penetrates into pores rather than just working on the surface. This makes it ideal for acne-prone, oily, and congested skin. BHAs are gentler and safer than comparable-strength AHAs, making them excellent for those prone to irritation. Best for: acne, blackheads, clogged pores, oily skin.

  • At-home range: 10–30% salicylic acid
  • Leave on for 2–5 minutes max

TCA Peels (Trichloroacetic Acid)

TCA is a medium-depth peel that penetrates deeper than AHAs or BHAs. It's used for more significant skin concerns: deeper wrinkles, acne scars, more severe hyperpigmentation. At 10–15%, it's cautiously accessible at home. At 20%+, it should only be used by professionals. TCA causes visible peeling (frosting) and significant downtime (5–10 days of peeling). Best for: acne scars, deeper wrinkles, significant pigmentation issues.

Warning: TCA at 20%+ used incorrectly at home has caused permanent scarring. Approach with extreme caution.

The Non-Negotiable Rules

  • Always patch test on your inner arm 48 hours before applying to your face
  • Never peel on active breakouts, sunburned, or irritated skin
  • Stop immediately if you experience intense burning, not just tingling — tingling is normal, burning is a sign to neutralize
  • Neutralize with baking soda + water if you need to stop the reaction
  • Never mix acids — don't do a glycolic peel and then apply a retinol the same night
  • SPF 30+ is mandatory the following day and week — peeled skin burns extremely easily
  • Wait at least 2–4 weeks between peels — more frequent isn't more effective, it's just damaging

Who Should Skip At-Home Peels

Don't do at-home chemical peels if you: are using tretinoin/retinoids (increase sensitivity dramatically), have rosacea or eczema, have very dark skin (Fitzpatrick V–VI — higher risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation), are pregnant, or have used isotretinoin (Accutane) in the last 6 months. These populations should consult a dermatologist for professionally supervised peels if they want acid treatments.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are at-home chemical peels safe?
At-home chemical peels are safe when used correctly — meaning the right concentration for your skin type, correct contact time, and never on compromised skin. Products containing 10–30% AHA or 2% BHA are appropriate for home use. Professional-strength peels (50–70% TCA) should only be administered by licensed providers. Always patch test first.
How often should I do an at-home chemical peel?
For most people, once per week is the maximum frequency for mild AHA peels. Stronger formulas like The Ordinary AHA 30% + BHA 2% should be used once every 10–14 days. Over-peeling is the most common mistake — more frequent peeling doesn't mean faster results; it means barrier damage.
Can I use a chemical peel if I have acne?
Yes — chemical peels can be beneficial for acne. BHA peels (salicylic acid) penetrate pores and help clear congestion. However, never peel active inflamed breakouts, broken skin, or areas of active redness. Always wait until active inflammation subsides before applying any exfoliant.

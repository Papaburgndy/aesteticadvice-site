How to Find a Good Med Spa Near You (And 5 Red Flags to Avoid)

Published: 2026-02-25 8 min read

The Med Spa Industry Has a Quality Problem (Here's How to Navigate It)

Med spas are booming. Walk into any strip mall in an affluent area and you'll find three new Botox clinics. The problem: there's no standardized regulation. Some are legitimate clinics run by board-certified dermatologists. Others are essentially unlicensed operations doing injectable procedures in a strip mall.

The med spa space attracts both legitimate medical professionals and dangerous charlatans. The difference between a great experience and a disfigured face is often just a few blocks away.

Here's how to tell the difference before you book your first appointment.

Red Flag #1: Medical Director Doesn't Actually Exist (Or Isn't Licensed)

The problem: Many states require med spas to have a medical director on staff—usually an MD or DO. Many clinics list a director who never actually works there. Some list someone with credentials that don't qualify (aesthetician ≠ doctor).

How to check:

  • Call and ask: "What days is Dr. [Name] in the clinic?" A legitimate answer is specific days/hours. If they dodge, that's suspicious.
  • Check state medical board websites (varies by state, but most have searchable registries). Look up the director's name. Verify they're currently licensed in your state.
  • Ask about the director's specialty. A board-certified dermatologist is ideal. A plastic surgeon is excellent. A family medicine doctor is acceptable. An unlicensed esthetician is a huge red flag.
  • Ask about the director's role: Do they supervise all injections? Do they do the injections themselves? If they just own the clinic and never appear, that's a sign they're not hands-on.

Why this matters: The medical director is legally responsible for the clinic's medical decisions. If something goes wrong, the director bears liability. If the director isn't actually there or isn't properly licensed, that's a disaster waiting to happen.

Red Flag #2: Injectors Without Proper Credentials

The problem: Who is actually injecting you? In legitimate clinics, it's either the medical director (MD/DO) or a licensed nurse practitioner (NP) or physician's assistant (PA) with specific training. In sketchy clinics, it might be an unlicensed "medical esthetician" with a 2-week online course.

How to check:

  • Ask: "What are the credentials of the person injecting me?" They should say "MD," "DO," "NP," or "PA." If they say "licensed esthetician" or "certified aesthetician," push back.
  • Ask to see proof. Legitimate clinics will show you credentials or be able to explain licensure quickly. If they get defensive, walk out.
  • Check the injector's background. Some states allow NPs/PAs to do injectables; others require physician supervision. Ask the clinic which applies in your state.
  • How much training? Legitimate injectors have taken specific botox/filler courses (often 1-2 days minimum), not just "esthetic training." Ask: "Where did you train for injectables?"

Why this matters: An untrained injector can cause nerve damage, vessel occlusion (which causes tissue death), asymmetry, and permanent disfigurement. You're trusting someone with your face. They need actual medical training.

Red Flag #3: Prices That Are Suspiciously Low

The problem: Botox costs money. Dermal fillers cost money. Legitimate clinics charge accordingly. If you see Botox for $99 or fillers for $299, something is off.

Realistic pricing:

  • Botox: $12-18 per unit. A typical forehead treatment is 20-30 units = $240-540. Anything cheaper suggests diluted product or unlicensed practice.
  • Dermal fillers: $600-1,200 per syringe depending on type. $300 for a syringe is a red flag.
  • Laser treatments: $400-2,000 depending on extent. Huge discounts suggest older equipment or undertrained operators.

How to check:

  • Call 3-5 clinics in your area. Get standard prices for your desired treatment. Is one clinic 50% cheaper than others? That's suspicious.
  • Be skeptical of Groupon med spa deals. Some legitimate clinics use Groupon, but many Groupon med spas are new or testing the market with low prices to build clientele. Evaluate carefully.
  • Ask about the product. "Is this name-brand Botox (Allergan) or generic Dysport?" Legitimate clinics will specify. Budget versions might use alternatives.

Why this matters: Low prices often mean low quality, diluted product, or unlicensed practice. You're saving $100 and risking your face. Not a good trade.

Red Flag #4: No Before-and-After Portfolio (Or Suspiciously Perfect Results)

The problem: Legitimate clinics should have photos of actual patients (consented) showing realistic results. If they have zero portfolio, that's suspicious. If they only show perfectly symmetrical, overfilled results, that's also suspicious (those often look unnatural).

How to check:

  • Ask to see a portfolio before booking. A good clinic will have 10-20 before-and-afters.
  • Look for variety. Different ages, skin types, concerns, and injection amounts. Identical results across all patients suggest either one injector (limiting skill range) or fake photos.
  • Look for natural-looking results. If everyone has huge, obvious lips or frozen foreheads, that tells you the clinic's aesthetic. Make sure it matches yours.
  • Check Google/Yelp reviews for patient photos. Real patients post results. If reviews mention natural-looking results, that's good. If they mention "looks way too done," that's a concern.

Why this matters: A portfolio is proof of skill. No portfolio means no proof. You're gambling.

Red Flag #5: Won't Discuss Your Allergies or Medical History

The problem: Injectables aren't completely risk-free. They interact with certain medications (blood thinners), certain conditions (autoimmune disorders), and certain allergies (lidocaine sensitivity in filler). A legitimate clinic will do a full intake.

How to check:

  • Does the clinic require a consultation before booking injections? If they just say "come in Tuesday and we'll inject you," walk out.
  • During consultation, are they asking about medications, allergies, medical history, previous procedures? Good sign if yes.
  • Do they mention risks and side effects? A good clinic will say "you might have bruising, asymmetry, or in rare cases, nerve damage." A bad clinic says "it's totally safe, nothing could go wrong."
  • Do they require informed consent? You should sign something acknowledging you understand the risks.

Why this matters: Botox and fillers can cause serious complications in people with certain conditions or on certain medications. A responsible clinic screens for this. A clinic that doesn't is cutting corners on safety.

How to Find a Good Med Spa: The Positive Indicators

1. Medical Director is hands-on: They actually work there, consult patients, and either do injections or directly supervise them.

2. Injectors are credentialed: MD, DO, NP, or PA with specific injectables training.

3. Pricing is realistic: Botox $12-18/unit, fillers $600-1,200/syringe, consistent with other clinics in your area.

4. Portfolio exists: 10-20 before-and-afters showing natural-looking results and variety.

5. Consultation is thorough: They ask medical history, discuss risks, require consent.

6. Reviews mention natural results: Google/Yelp reviews say things like "looks refreshed, not overdone" rather than "obvious Botox."

7. They're honest about limitations: A good clinic will say "you're not a good candidate for fillers due to your face shape" rather than upselling you on everything.

Finding Verified Providers

Use resources like Clinovyr.com (a directory of verified aesthetic providers) to find clinics that have been credentialed and vetted. This removes some of the guesswork.

Also check:

  • Allergan Certified Provider: Botox manufacturer maintains a list of certified injectors. Being on this list is a good sign.
  • Board certification: Look for "board-certified dermatologist," "board-certified plastic surgeon," etc. This is the gold standard.
  • State medical board: Verify the medical director and any doctor-level staff are in good standing with your state's medical board.

The Honest Bottom Line

Good med spas do exist. They're run by board-certified doctors with trained injectors and reasonable prices. But so do terrible ones that are dangerous.

Before you book, do due diligence: verify credentials, ask questions, look at portfolios, and trust your gut. If something feels sketchy, it probably is.

The "good deal" that costs 30% less might cost you your face. That's not a deal. It's a disaster.

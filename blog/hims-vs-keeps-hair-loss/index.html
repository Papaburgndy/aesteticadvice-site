Hims vs Keeps: Which Hair Loss Treatment Actually Works in 2026?

Published: 2026-03-05 8 min read

The Battle for Hair Loss Treatment Dominance

If you're losing hair and considering an online telehealth service, you've probably stumbled across both Hims and Keeps. Both companies dominate the hair loss space with slick marketing and impressive before-and-after photos. But which one actually delivers better results, and more importantly, which offers better value for your money?

We've tested both services extensively, and the answer is more nuanced than you might think. Both work—but they work differently, and the right choice depends on your specific situation, budget, and preferences.

The Active Ingredients: They're More Similar Than You'd Think

Here's the reality: Hims and Keeps both prescribe the same two FDA-approved ingredients for hair loss—finasteride (Propecia) and minoxidil (Rogaine). These are the only two topical/oral treatments that consistently prevent hair loss and regrow hair in clinical studies.

Finasteride (5-1mg daily): A DHT inhibitor that addresses the root cause of male pattern baldness. Works best when started early. You need a prescription.

Minoxidil (topical, 5% solution): Stimulates hair follicles and extends the growth phase. Available over-the-counter, but both services include it. More effective when combined with finasteride.

The difference isn't in the chemistry—it's in the delivery, support, and pricing. Let's break that down.

Pricing Showdown: Keeps Edges Out Hims for Most Users

Hims charges $40/month for generic finasteride (after an initial $30-40 consultation), or roughly $480/year for ongoing treatment. Minoxidil is an additional $45/month. So a combination treatment runs you about $1,020 annually.

Keeps pricing is slightly better structured: $30/month for finasteride + minoxidil combination, or $50/month if you want brand-name options. That's $360-600/year, significantly cheaper than Hims for equivalent treatment.

Winner: Keeps for price-conscious patients. Hims for people already using other Hims services (they may bundle discounts).

Subscription Flexibility and Cancellation

This is where real-world usability matters. Keeps allows you to pause or cancel anytime without penalty. You can order a 3-month supply upfront and pause for 2 months if you're concerned about side effects or just want to test the waters.

Hims works on a similar model, but the user experience is less intuitive. You're technically locked into monthly deliveries unless you actively cancel, which feels more like a traditional subscription trap than Keeps' cleaner approach.

Winner: Keeps for flexibility.

The Consultation and Ongoing Support

Both services use licensed doctors for initial consultations, and both handle this via text/chat. Hims has more brand recognition and may feel more established, which some patients prefer psychologically.

Keeps' doctors are equally qualified, but Keeps leans harder into the community angle—you get access to support forums and educational content beyond just your prescription. If you value educational resources about hair loss science, Keeps has the edge.

For finasteride, both require an initial consultation ($30-40). For minoxidil, no consultation is needed since it's OTC.

Results Timeline: Patience is Required

Both services will tell you this upfront: you won't see results in weeks. Here's the honest timeline:

Months 1-3: Hair shedding may actually increase (normal). You're shedding old hair to make room for new growth.

Months 4-6: Subtle improvement. Fine hair growth visible at the hairline if you're looking closely.

Months 6-12: Noticeable thickening. People around you start noticing improvement.

12+ months: Maximum results plateau around month 12-18. Finasteride prevents further loss; minoxidil regrows what was lost.

This timeline is identical between Hims and Keeps because the medications are the same. The difference is whether you stick with it, and that comes down to support and price.

Product Range and Customization

Hims offers a slightly wider range of formulations: you can get finasteride + minoxidil combinations, or add topical treatments like tretinoin if you're also concerned about skin health. They're positioning themselves as a broader wellness platform.

Keeps is more narrowly focused: they specialize in hair loss with excellent execution. If hair loss is your only concern, Keeps' simplified approach is cleaner. If you want to address multiple concerns through one provider, Hims' broader catalog wins.

Side Effects and Safety Monitoring

Both require baseline health information to ensure finasteride is safe for you (it's contraindicated for those with prostate issues or certain liver conditions). Both provide follow-up consultations to monitor for side effects.

Common side effects of finasteride: reduced sex drive (2-3% of users), erectile dysfunction (rare), breast tenderness (very rare). Minoxidil: scalp irritation, dizziness if absorbed systemically (minimal).

Both services handle this fine, though Keeps' educational content makes users slightly more aware of what to expect.

The Verdict: Which Should You Choose?

Choose Keeps if: You want the best value for hair loss treatment, prefer flexibility in subscriptions, appreciate educational resources, and want simplicity.

Choose Hims if: You want to bundle hair loss treatment with other health concerns (ED, skincare, general wellness), or if you already use other Hims services and can get bundled discounts.

The truth is both work. The actual medication is the same. What varies is the experience and cost. For a pure hair loss focus, Keeps is the smarter financial choice. For integrated wellness management, Hims has broader reach.

Our recommendation: Start with Keeps for hair loss. After 3-6 months, reassess whether you need additional services. If you do, Hims' ecosystem becomes more attractive.

Important: Both services require ongoing use. If you stop finasteride, you'll lose the regained hair within 6-12 months. This isn't a permanent cure—it's ongoing management, like blood pressure medication. That's not a weakness of either service; it's reality.

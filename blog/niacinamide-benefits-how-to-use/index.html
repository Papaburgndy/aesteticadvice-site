Niacinamide in Skincare: Benefits, How to Use It, and Best Products

By AesteticAdvice Editors · Updated 2026-03-22 · ⏱ 5 min read · ✅ Expert Reviewed
Disclosure: AesteticAdvice may earn a commission from Amazon and other retailers when you use our links — at no extra cost to you. We never accept payment for reviews or rankings.

Niacinamide works through multiple pathways simultaneously, which is why it has such a broad range of published benefits:

  • Pore appearance: Regulates sebum production, reducing the oiliness that makes pores look enlarged. Results visible in 4 weeks.
  • Dark spots: Inhibits the transfer of melanosomes (melanin packets) from melanocytes to surrounding skin cells, reducing hyperpigmentation.
  • Barrier function: Stimulates ceramide and fatty acid synthesis in the stratum corneum, strengthening the skin's moisture barrier.
  • Anti-inflammatory: Reduces redness and inflammation associated with acne and rosacea.
  • Collagen: Some evidence for stimulating collagen and keratin production at higher concentrations.

What Concentration Do You Need?

  • 2–5%: Good for beginners and sensitive skin. Barrier support, gentle brightening.
  • 5–10%: Optimal for most people. Clinical studies showing pore and dark spot improvement typically use 5–10%.
  • Above 10%: Minimal additional benefit for most goals; may cause flushing in some people.

FAQ

Can I use niacinamide with retinol?
Yes — niacinamide actually helps buffer retinol irritation. Using niacinamide in the morning and retinol at night is a classic combination. Some people use them together in the evening with niacinamide applied first.
Can niacinamide cause skin purging?
No — purging is associated with actives that accelerate cell turnover (retinoids, AHAs, BHAs). Niacinamide doesn't accelerate turnover, so breakouts after starting niacinamide are a reaction, not purging. This can happen if the formula contains other ingredients your skin doesn't tolerate.
Niacinamide vs vitamin C for dark spots?
Both work but through different mechanisms — vitamin C inhibits melanin production while niacinamide blocks melanin transfer to skin cells. Combined use is more effective than either alone. Use vitamin C in the AM and niacinamide in the PM or together if your skin tolerates it.
